# Strategic Reversal

Use this skill when a scene's appeal comes from strategy, not brute force. The win should feel surprising in the moment and fair in hindsight.

## Core Pattern

1. **Scene Game / Contest**: Define what is being won or lost. This can be a literal game, fight, trial, negotiation, social trap, chase, debate, or puzzle.
2. **Visible Rules**: Make the rules clear enough for the reader to follow the pressure.
3. **Stakes**: Show what changes if the character wins or loses.
4. **Opponent Belief**: Identify what the opponent thinks is true.
5. **Hidden Lever**: Identify the overlooked rule, assumption, weakness, clue, or social read that enables the reversal.
6. **Fairness Seed**: Plant at least one clue before the payoff. The reader should be able to look back and see that the answer was present.
7. **Reversal**: Reveal the move that changes the board state.
8. **Cost / Vulnerability**: Give the win a cost, risk, emotional exposure, or new problem.
9. **Aftermath**: Show how the result changes status, relationships, resources, or future threats.

## Writing Rules

- Explain rules through action, pressure, dialogue, or conflict. Avoid dry rule dumps.
- Let the protagonist observe, test, bluff, or provoke before revealing the answer.
- Do not make the character look smart by making everyone else stupid. Give the opponent a reasonable plan.
- Tie the character's coolness to a specific weakness, obsession, wound, or limitation.
- Keep the explanation shorter than the tension that earned it.
- A reversal is strongest when it changes both the plot state and the emotional/social state.

## Editing Checks

Flag the scene if:

- The reversal depends on information the reader could not possibly know.
- The win has no cost, risk, or consequence.
- The opponent ignores an obvious countermeasure.
- The explanation arrives as a long lecture after tension has already died.
- The scene is clever but emotionally empty.
- The tactic contradicts established character ability, world rules, or glossary terms.
