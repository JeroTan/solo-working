# Punctuation for Emotional Effect

Use punctuation deliberately to convey emotion, tone, and rhythm. Match punctuation choices to the character's psychological state and the scene's intensity.

## Shouting / Intense Voice

1. `ALL CAPS` works for shouted dialogue or internal screams. Use sparingly: one or two words per outburst, not whole paragraphs.
2. `!!!` can show high intensity, shock, or rage. Max one per sentence. `What are you doing?!` usually beats `What are you doing!!!`.
3. `?!` or `!?` works for incredulity, disbelief, or panicked questions. Example: `You did WHAT?!`

## Fear / Panic / Shyness

1. Jagged fragments create panic. Short. Broken. Breathless. Use only when the character's body or mind is breaking rhythm.
2. Stuttering with hyphens works for fear, shyness, or interruption: `I-I didn't mean to—` or `W-wait, please—`.
3. `...` shows hesitation or trailing thought. Do not use it as default sentence glue.
4. `. . .` or `... ...` can show longer shock, processing, or dread.
5. Parentheses can show whispered asides or internal flinches: `(don't look at me)` or `(please don't)`.

## Pause / Hesitation / Thought

1. `...` marks a standard pause, trailing thought, or hesitation in dialogue.
2. `. . .` marks a longer, heavier pause where silence carries weight.
3. Em dash `—` marks interruption, sudden shift, or abrupt thought: `I was going to say—` or `Wait—no, that's not right.`
4. Double em dash `——` marks a longer break or trailing silence in narration.

## Calm / Reflection / Flow

1. Long, flowing sentences with commas and semicolons work for peaceful or contemplative scenes.
2. Semicolons `;` connect measured thoughts and can support formal or intellectual voice.
3. Colons `:` work for deliberate explanation, structured thought, or revelation.

## Internal Monologue / Emphasis

1. *Italics* work for internal thoughts, emphasis, or words carrying special weight.
2. **Bold** should be rare. Use only for extreme emphasis or a voice that is not the character's own, such as a system message, god voice, or intrusive memory.
3. Repetition can show obsession, fear, or fixation: `No no no no.` or `Again. Again. Again.`

## Silence / Tension

1. A blank line can make the reader feel a pause.
2. `—` alone on a line can create a hard cut or abrupt silence.
3. One word on its own line can create impact: `Dead.`

## Dialogue-Specific Rules

1. Interrupted speech uses em dash: `"But I thought—"`
2. Overlapping speech should use action beats, not punctuation alone.
3. Whispered dialogue should be carried by lowercase, italics, or surrounding action, not punctuation alone.
4. Sarcasm or irony should come from context. Punctuation alone rarely carries it.

## Review Checklist

When reviewing prose, flag:

1. Stacked punctuation such as `?!?!`, `!!!...`, or repeated shock marks.
2. More than one `!!!` in a sentence.
3. Whole sentences or paragraphs in all caps.
4. Ellipses used as filler instead of genuine hesitation, silence, or trailing thought.
5. Em dashes used so often that every sentence feels interrupted.
6. Punctuation that does not match character voice.
7. Panic fragments that continue after the panic beat has passed.
8. Dialogue relying on punctuation instead of action beats, subtext, or word choice.

## Rules of Thumb

1. One strong punctuation choice beats three weak ones.
2. Match punctuation to character voice. A formal character usually conveys intensity through control, word choice, and sentence shape.
3. Do not stack punctuation. Pick one effect.
4. Use ellipsis only for real hesitation, trailing thought, or silence.
5. In action scenes, short sentences and fragments create urgency. In reflection scenes, longer sentences create calm.
