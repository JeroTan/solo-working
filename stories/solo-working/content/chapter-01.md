# Chapter 1 — The Weakest in the Office

*There are people in this world who are born to lead. I have met several of them. They sat two rows ahead of me and never learned my name.*

---

The alarm went off at 6:14 AM.

Juan dela Cruz did not need it. He had been awake since 5:47, lying flat on his back in the dark, staring at the ceiling of his seven-square-meter studio unit in Pasay, cataloguing everything that could go wrong today.

The list was long.

He rose without ceremony, folded his blanket with the precision of a man who had learned early that no one else would do it for him, and began his preparations for battle.

He ironed his shirt. The same white button-down he had worn on Mondays for the past two years — not because he lacked others, but because white was forgettable. Forgettable was strategic. In an office where visibility meant accountability, and accountability meant being assigned more work with no additional compensation, invisibility was the only armor a Rank-F employee could afford.

He ate breakfast standing over the kitchen sink. Rice from last night. One fried egg. No salt. Salt cost forty pesos a pack and he had priorities.

He checked his bag: laptop charger, notebook, two black ballpen, one backup ballpen. He did not trust the pens in the office supply cabinet. They ran out at the worst moments. He had learned this on his third day, during a client call he was supposed to be taking minutes for, and he had not forgotten.

Juan dela Cruz did not forget things.

It was the only skill he had.

---

The MRT was a dungeon.

Not metaphorically. Not yet — though that understanding would come later, in circumstances he could not currently imagine. But pragmatically: it was dark in the tunnels, it was dangerous in the crowd, and the only way through it was to keep moving and not show weakness.

He boarded at Taft Avenue at 7:03 AM, pressed against seventeen strangers with the quiet resignation of someone who had made peace with discomfort long ago. He used the twenty-two minutes to review the day's agenda in his notebook.

His handwriting was small. Controlled. Each item listed in order of urgency. Beside each item: an estimated completion time. Beside that: a contingency.

People who saw the notebook thought he was odd.

People who didn't see the notebook thought he was slow.

Neither group had ever missed a deadline.

He arrived at the Pinnacle Systems Inc. building at 7:48 AM. Twelve minutes before his shift officially began. He always arrived twelve minutes early. Not because the employee handbook suggested it, and not because anyone had noticed or rewarded him for it in two years.

He arrived early because arriving early meant choosing his seat before the air conditioning became a contested resource, and because it gave him time to read his emails before anyone could intercept him with a verbal request — the corporate equivalent of an ambush — and because in the two years he had worked at Pinnacle Systems, he had learned that the day did not wait for anyone.

Especially not for Rank-F employees.

---

The 11th floor was already lit when Juan stepped off the elevator.

Fluorescent. Merciless. The kind of light that had no interest in making anyone look good. It illuminated every surface with equal indifference: the motivational poster by the entrance (*EXCELLENCE IS A HABIT — Management*), the dying potted plant no one had watered since the previous quarter, and Juan dela Cruz, Rank-F Associate, IT Services Division, logging in to his workstation at 7:49 AM.

He navigated to the shared drive. Located the files he had prepped yesterday. Opened the task tracker.

Seven items. Three of them had been added after 6 PM last night by people who were no longer in the building.

He updated the tracker. Reprioritized. Recalculated.

*Another day.*

The office filled gradually around him, the way water fills a container — slowly, then all at once. By 8:30 AM, the floor was at capacity. Seventy-three employees across four departments, none of whom had greeted Juan dela Cruz, and only two of whom knew his full name.

He knew all of theirs.

He had learned them systematically during his first month, cross-referenced against the organizational chart, and mapped each name to a face, a department, a rank, and a pattern of behavior. Knowledge was the only currency a person with no rank could freely accumulate.

He was, by the company's official assessment, a Rank-F employee.

The ranking system at Pinnacle Systems had originated three years ago as a "gamification initiative" proposed by the HR department to improve employee engagement. It was based on quarterly performance evaluations, client feedback scores, and a proprietary algorithm no one fully understood, including the people who had built it.

Rank S: The untouchables. Two people in the entire company. They existed somewhere above the 14th floor in the realm of executive decisions and business-class flights, and their names were spoken in the 11th floor breakroom with the kind of reverence normally reserved for natural disasters.

Rank A: The ascended. Senior managers and principal architects. Six of them. They had parking slots.

Rank B through D: The functioning middle. Project leads, senior associates, the people who knew where the template files were stored.

Rank E: The hopeful. Junior employees trending upward. Twelve months of decent performance reviews away from something better.

Rank F: The floor.

There were nine Rank-F employees in Pinnacle Systems. Juan dela Cruz had been one of them for seven hundred and thirty-one days.

He was not trending upward.

---

At 9:15 AM, his manager, Dennis Soriano, arrived.

Dennis Soriano was a Rank-C Project Manager, forty-one years old, with the energy of a man who had once had ambitions and had since negotiated a comfortable truce with their absence. He wore collared shirts in varying shades of blue. He smelled faintly of Jollibee breakfast. He was, in the taxonomy of office obstacles, a *passive-aggressive senior* — not actively hostile, but possessed of an extraordinary talent for delegating work to whichever subordinate was least likely to complain.

Juan dela Cruz was that subordinate.

"Juan," Dennis said, setting his bag down without looking up. "Did you get my messages last night?"

"Yes."

"Good. The Reyes account report needs reformatting before ten. The client template changed."

"I saw. I've already started."

Dennis looked up. His expression was that of a man who had expected a problem and received instead a solution, and was not sure how to feel about it. He made a small sound — something between acknowledgment and dismissal — and turned on his computer.

Juan returned to his screen.

The Reyes account report took forty-seven minutes. The new client template had been designed, Juan noted without comment, by someone who had never been required to actually fill one out. He rebuilt it from scratch, mapped the data fields, checked the formulas, triple-checked the executive summary, and submitted it at 9:58 AM.

Two minutes early.

He noted this in his notebook as a matter of record.

No one acknowledged it.

He had stopped expecting acknowledgment on day ninety-four.

---

The announcement came at 2:00 PM.

Juan was in the middle of his lunch — instant noodles, consumed at his desk, twelve minutes flat — when the calendar notification appeared:

**ALL-HANDS MEETING — IT SERVICES DIV — CONF. ROOM 11-A — 2:00 PM**
*Attendance: Mandatory*
*Agenda: PROJECT SKYFALL KICKOFF*

He closed his notebook.

He looked at the notification for three seconds.

Then he ate the last of his noodles, threw away the cup, and walked to Conference Room 11-A.

---

The conference room smelled of stale coffee and old decisions.

Fourteen people filed in and arranged themselves in the unconscious hierarchy that governs all office seating: Rank A and B nearest the screen, where the projector's light might illuminate them favorably; Rank C and D in the secure middle; Rank E along the sides, angled for optimal visibility; and Rank F wherever was left.

Juan took the seat nearest the door.

The projector lagged for ninety seconds before the presentation loaded. The deck was forty-three slides long. The cover slide read: *PROJECT SKYFALL: ENTERPRISE SYSTEM MIGRATION — PHASE 1 KICKOFF*.

The man at the front of the room was not Dennis Soriano. Dennis Soriano was sitting three seats to Juan's right with the fixed expression of someone who had received information slightly earlier than everyone else and was now managing his reaction.

The man at the front was Raymond Lim, Rank-A Vice President of Operations. He was fifty-three. He had the pleasant, weathered confidence of someone who had survived enough corporate catastrophes to stop fearing the new ones.

He smiled at the assembled room.

"Thank you all for coming on short notice," Raymond Lim said. "I'll be brief."

He was not brief.

For the next twenty-two minutes, he explained that Pinnacle Systems had been contracted to migrate its largest client's enterprise infrastructure to a new cloud system. The client had set a completion deadline of forty-five days. The project had originally been scheduled to begin in the third quarter of next year.

The client had moved the deadline forward.

By eleven months.

The room was very quiet as this information settled.

Juan wrote the deadline in his notebook. Drew a small box around it. Calculated backward from the date, mapping dependencies. He filled two-thirds of a page in the time it took Raymond Lim to finish explaining the compensation structure, which was: none, beyond standard salary, as this fell within the scope of existing contractual obligations.

The room remained quiet. But it was a different kind of quiet now. The kind that had weight to it.

"The team has been pre-selected," Raymond Lim continued, clicking to the next slide.

A list of names. Eight of them.

Juan read the list from the bottom up — a habit he had developed for finding his own name in documents where it almost never appeared at the top.

There it was. Last position.

*J. dela Cruz — Associate, IT Services*

He looked at the other seven names. Recognized each one. Mapped each immediately to their specialty and their current workload.

He ran the calculation quickly, silently, in the margin of his notebook.

Eight people. Forty-five days. An enterprise migration of this scale required a minimum team of thirty, working in three rotating shifts, with a dedicated QA track and a fallback protocol.

They had eight.

One of whom was him.

He capped his pen.

*This,* he thought, with the calm clarity of someone who has identified a threat and accepted that it is real, *is going to be very bad.*

He did not know, sitting in that room, with the projector humming and the whiteboard markers all running low and the stale coffee slowly cooling in its paper cup, that he was about to survive something that would break every other person in that building.

He did not know about the System.

He did not know about the window that would appear in the dark, three weeks from now, when the servers had crashed and the data had corrupted and he was the only one left.

He did not know about any of it yet.

He only knew the deadline.

Forty-five days.

He turned to a fresh page.

He began to plan.

*End of Chapter 1*
