# Chapter 2 — The Grind Before the Fall

*A deadline is not a suggestion. It is a timer on a bomb. And in the corporate world, everyone is sitting on it while pretending it's an ergonomic chair.*

---

The first fourteen days of Project Skyfall were a blur of fluorescent lights, lukewarm coffee, and the sound of seventy-two fingers tapping against plastic keyboards.

The office had transformed. The 11th floor, once a place of predictable misery, was now a war room. Tables were pushed together. Whiteboards were covered in scribbled diagrams that looked more like occult symbols than network architecture. The air was thick with the smell of ozone from the overworked servers and the desperation of people who hadn't slept more than five hours a night since the kickoff.

Juan dela Cruz was exactly where he always was.

Station 11-47. The corner seat. The one nearest the door.

He was the "documentation lead" for the migration — a title that, in practice, meant he was responsible for recording every mistake the senior architects made so they could blame the system later.

He was working on a 400-page technical manual for the new cloud environment. It was a task that required the focus of a monk and the patience of a saint. Juan had both, mostly because he had nothing else.

"Juan," a voice snapped.

He didn't look up immediately. He finished the sentence he was typing — *Section 4.2.1: Redundancy Protocols* — and then turned.

It was Rodel Magsino.

In the office hierarchy, Rodel was a Rank-B Senior Analyst. He was thirty-two, possessed of a genius-level understanding of data structures, and currently looked like he was being slowly digested by his own stress. His eyes were bloodshot. His tie was loose. He was holding a stack of printouts like they were the evidence of a crime.

"Yes, Rodel?" Juan asked.

"The mapping for the legacy database is failing," Rodel said, his voice tight. "The field lengths don't match. If we don't fix the schema by midnight, the batch migration will corrupt everything. Everything, Juan."

Juan looked at the printouts. He had already seen the schema three days ago. He had noted the field length discrepancy in his notebook. He had even sent an email about it to the Project Manager, Dennis Soriano.

"I sent an email about this on Tuesday," Juan said quietly.

Rodel blinked. "You did?"

"CC'd to you and Dennis. Subject: *⚠ Schema Mismatch — Database Migration Track*."

Rodel stared at him. Then he looked at his phone. He scrolled. He found the email. It was unread.

"Putangina," Rodel whispered. It wasn't an insult; it was a prayer. He looked at Juan, and for the first time in two years, he actually seemed to see him. "Why didn't you say anything in the standup?"

"I tried," Juan said. "I was Item 8 on the agenda. We ran out of time at Item 4."

Rodel let out a jagged breath. "Right. Right. Everyone was too busy arguing about the color of the dashboard icons. Okay. Okay, I'll fix it. I have to stay. Again."

"I can help with the data entry," Juan offered. "I've already mapped the first fifty tables in my personal notes."

Rodel looked at Juan's notebook. It was open to a page of perfectly aligned columns and rows.

"You're a Rank-F, right?" Rodel asked, his voice softened by exhaustion.

"For seven hundred and forty-five days," Juan replied.

Rodel shook his head. "This place is a joke. A sick, broken joke."

He walked away, back into the maze of monitors, leaving Juan alone in the corner.

---

Day 21 was the breaking point.

It started with a sound. Not a loud one. Just a soft *beep* from the server room, followed by the collective silence of fourteen people realizing that the internet had just died.

And with the internet, the migration.

The cloud synchronization was at 84%. It had been running for thirty-six hours. If it stopped now without a graceful shutdown, the data parity would be lost. Millions of records — customer IDs, transaction histories, encrypted passwords — would become digital noise.

"Nobody touch anything!" Dennis Soriano screamed, standing up so fast his chair flipped over. "What happened? Who tripped the breaker?"

"It's not the breaker!" someone yelled from the back. "The server rack is overheating! The AC in the server room failed!"

The 11th floor descended into chaos.

It was the kind of chaos that doesn't involve running or screaming, but rather the frantic, high-pitched energy of people who are watching their entire lives' work evaporate in real-time.

Dennis was on the phone, yelling at Facility Management. Rodel was at his desk, his hands over his face, shaking. Cynthia Ocampo, the Rank-B Presentation Queen, was staring at her screen where a "CRITICAL ERROR" window had appeared, her perfectly manicured nails digging into the armrests of her chair.

Juan dela Cruz sat still.

He looked at his notebook. He looked at his watch. 11:43 PM.

He stood up.

He didn't go to the server room. He didn't join the shouting match around Dennis's desk. Instead, he walked to the back of the office, to the old storage closet that no one used.

He knew what was in there. A portable industrial fan. It had been left there by a contractor three years ago. He also knew that the server room had a secondary ventilation duct that had been boarded up during the last renovation, but was still accessible if you knew which ceiling tile to move.

He moved through the office like a ghost. No one noticed him. No one asked what he was doing.

He dragged the fan to the server room door. It was locked.

"The key!" Dennis was screaming. "Who has the server room key? Aldrich has it! Where is Aldrich?"

Aldrich Santos, the Senior Manager, was not in the office. He had left at 6:00 PM for a "networking dinner" with the client.

The temperature in the server room was climbing. 38 degrees. 40 degrees. The servers were beginning their emergency thermal shutdowns.

Juan looked at the door. Then he looked at the ceiling.

He climbed onto a nearby desk — Rodel's desk. Rodel didn't even notice. He was currently experiencing what looked like a full-blown panic attack, staring into the middle distance and whispering about "lost packets."

Juan pushed aside a ceiling tile. The air that came out was hot enough to make him sweat instantly. He reached in, found the latch for the old duct, and pulled.

With a groan of protesting metal, the duct opened.

Juan positioned the industrial fan. He plugged it in. He aimed it directly into the duct.

*VROOOOOM.*

The fan roared to life, pushing a column of relatively cooler office air into the heat-soaked server room.

For a moment, nothing happened.

Then, the temperature display on the door began to tick down.

42... 41... 40...

"It's dropping!" someone shouted. "The temp is dropping! How?"

Juan didn't answer. He climbed down from the desk. He walked back to his station. He sat down.

He had bought them ten minutes. Maybe fifteen. But it wasn't enough. The synchronization was still stalled. The data was still corrupted.

The breaking wasn't physical. It was mental.

One by one, the team began to fail.

It started with the junior associates. They just... stopped. They sat back, their faces blank, their eyes glazed. Then the seniors. Cynthia started to cry — quiet, shuddering sobs that she tried to hide behind her hands.

Then Rodel.

Rodel Magsino, the genius, the anchor of the team, stood up. He looked at his screen. He looked at the chaos around him. And then he did something he had never done in his entire professional career.

He took his employee ID badge, laid it flat on his desk, and walked out of the office.

He didn't say a word. He just left.

"Rodel!" Dennis yelled. "Rodel, where are you going? We need the mapping! Rodel!"

But Rodel was gone.

The migration was dead. The project was over. Pinnacle Systems was going to lose its biggest client, and fourteen people were going to lose their jobs.

The lights in the office began to flicker.

It wasn't the power. It was something else. A static charge in the air that made the hair on Juan's arms stand up.

The screens on every workstation began to glitch. The "CRITICAL ERROR" windows didn't just stay on the monitors — they began to bleed out, the blue light of the screens intensifying until it filled the room.

Juan felt a pressure in his chest. A weight, like someone was pressing a hand against his heart.

He looked around.

Everyone else was frozen. Literally. Dennis was mid-shout, his mouth open, a bead of sweat hanging from his chin. Cynthia was a statue of grief. The clock on the wall had stopped at 11:58:43 PM.

Silence. Absolute, terrifying silence.

And then, a window appeared.

It wasn't on a screen. It was in the air, floating three inches in front of Juan's face. It was semi-transparent, glowing with a soft, corporate blue light.

```
╔══════════════════════════════════════════╗
║           SYSTEM INITIALIZATION          ║
║                                          ║
║  The work environment has reached        ║
║  CRITICAL CAPACITY.                      ║
║                                          ║
║  A new Worker has been detected.         ║
║  Name: Juan dela Cruz                    ║
║  Rank: F                                 ║
║                                          ║
║  Do you accept the terms and conditions? ║
║  (Failure to accept will result in       ║
║  immediate termination of the project.)  ║
║                                          ║
║        [ ACCEPT ]      [ DECLINE ]       ║
╚══════════════════════════════════════════╝
```

Juan looked at the window.

He didn't panic. He didn't scream. He didn't even blink.

He had spent two years being ignored. He had spent two years being the "Weakest in the Office." He had spent two years watching the system fail him.

He looked at the [ ACCEPT ] button.

"Will there be a benefits package?" he asked the empty air.

The System did not answer.

Juan reached out and pressed [ ACCEPT ].

A new window bloomed, larger and brighter than the first.

```
╔══════════════════════════════════════════╗
║            TRIAL QUEST TRIGGERED         ║
║                                          ║
║  QUEST: THE IMPOSSIBLE MIGRATION         ║
║  OBJECTIVE: Recover 100% of data parity. ║
║  TIME LIMIT: 00:01:17                    ║
║                                          ║
║  REWARD: Unique Skill Unlock.            ║
║  PENALTY: Resignation (Physical Death).  ║
╚══════════════════════════════════════════╝
```

Juan looked at the timer.

*00:01:15... 00:01:14...*

He didn't have the mapping. He didn't have the access codes. He didn't have the bandwidth.

He looked at his notebook.

*Page 47. Database Schema.*

He looked at the frozen office. At the broken people.

"I can't do this alone," he whispered.

The System pulsed.

```
╔══════════════════════════════════════════╗
║         UNIQUE SKILL TEMPORARILY         ║
║                ACTIVATED                 ║
║                                          ║
║               SKILL: RAISE               ║
║                                          ║
║  Target the broken.                      ║
║  Target the fallen.                      ║
║  Command them.                           ║
╚══════════════════════════════════════════╝
```

Juan looked at Rodel's empty chair. At Cynthia's frozen face.

He didn't know what he was doing. He didn't know the words. But he felt a pull in his shadow, a weight that wanted to expand, to reach out and pull something back from the edge of the void.

He looked at the server room, where the heat was rising again.

He stood up.

"Raise," he said.

It wasn't a shout. It was a command.

From the shadows beneath Rodel's desk, something began to move. Something dark, flickering like static, taking the shape of a man who looked exactly like Rodel Magsino, but with eyes that burned with the cold light of a thousand spreadsheets.

The Shadow Rodel looked at Juan. It bowed.

*"Awaiting data, boss,"* the shadow whispered.

Juan looked at the timer.

*00:00:42...*

"Fix the mapping," Juan said. "Now."

The shadow blurred toward the keyboard.
